## Executive Summary

This comprehensive literature review examines neural invasion in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), focusing on phenotypic classifications, pathological mechanisms, and the distinct roles of different neuron types. Over the past two decades, research has transformed our understanding from viewing perineural invasion (PNI) as a passive histological finding to recognizing it as an active, bidirectional process with significant therapeutic implications.

**Key Findings:**
- PNI occurs in 70-100% of PDAC cases, the highest rate among solid tumors
- Three neuron types (sympathetic, parasympathetic, sensory) have distinct and sometimes opposing roles
- Sympathetic and sensory neurons promote tumor progression; parasympathetic signaling appears protective
- Multiple molecular pathways (neurotrophins, axon guidance molecules, chemokines) drive PNI
- Emerging therapeutic strategies target neural signaling with promising preclinical results

## 1. Introduction and Clinical Significance

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is one of the most lethal malignancies, characterized by aggressive local invasion, early metastasis, and profound resistance to therapy. Among its distinctive pathological features, **perineural invasion (PNI)** stands out with a prevalence of **70-100%**, surpassing any other solid tumor (Gasparini et al., 2019).

### 1.1 Definition of PNI

PNI is defined as the infiltration of neoplastic cells along nerve fibers and within nerve sheaths, including:
- **Epineural space** - outermost connective tissue layer
- **Perineural space** - middle layer surrounding nerve fascicles
- **Endoneural space** - innermost layer around individual nerve fibers

### 1.2 Clinical Impact

PNI represents an independent prognostic factor associated with:

| Clinical Outcome | Impact of PNI |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Local recurrence | Significantly increased |
| Metastatic potential | Enhanced |
| Cancer-associated pain | Severe, intractable |
| Overall survival | Diminished |
| R1 resection rate | Increased |

Recent advances in cancer neuroscience have revealed that the relationship between PDAC and nerves is **bidirectional**: cancer cells actively invade nerves while simultaneously reprogramming the neural microenvironment to support tumor progression (Thiel et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2021).

## 2. Phenotypic Classifications of Neural Invasion

### 2.1 PNI vs. Neural Remodeling

The literature distinguishes between two related but distinct phenomena:

| Feature | Perineural Invasion (PNI) | Neural Remodeling |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **Definition** | Cancer cells invading nerve sheaths | Structural/functional changes in nerves |
| **Process** | Active tumor cell migration along nerves | Nerve sprouting, hypertrophy, altered density |
| **Markers** | Tumor cells within perineural space | Changes in nerve fiber density, caliber |
| **Clinical Impact** | Route for metastasis | Supports tumor growth, pain generation |

Neural remodeling encompasses:
- **Axonogenesis** - new nerve fiber formation
- **Neurogenesis** - new neuron formation  
- **Neural hypertrophy** - increased nerve size

A landmark 2025 study using "Trace-n-Seq" technology revealed that PDAC reprograms innervating neurons at the single-cell level, identifying novel neuronal subtypes specific to the tumor microenvironment (Thiel et al., 2025).

### 2.2 Six Steps of PNI

Recent work has defined six specific steps of PNI (Sun et al., 2024):

```
Step 1: Nerve-tumor proximity
    ↓
Step 2: Perineural barrier disruption (TGFβ1-driven EMT)
    ↓
Step 3: Cancer cell attachment (L1CAM, NCAM-mediated)
    ↓
Step 4: Invasion initiation (chemotactic gradients)
    ↓
Step 5: Intraneuronal migration
    ↓
Step 6: Distant dissemination
```

### 2.3 Neural Injury-Repair Phenotype

A critical discovery is that PDAC induces **neural injury**, triggering a repair response in peripheral neuroglia (particularly Schwann cells) (Weitz et al., 2023). This "repair signature" paradoxically promotes PNI by:

1. Activating Schwann cell migration toward tumor
2. Releasing neurotrophic factors
3. Creating a permissive microenvironment for cancer cell invasion

## 3. Pathological Types and Molecular Mechanisms

### 3.1 Molecular Mediators of PNI

#### 3.1.1 Neurotrophins and Their Receptors

| Factor | Source | Receptor | Function in PNI |
|--------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **NGF** | Tumor cells, nerves | TrkA, p75NTR | Promotes neurotropism, pain |
| **GDNF** | Nerves, Schwann cells | RET, GFRα1 | Major chemoattractant for PDAC cells |
| **BDNF** | Tumor cells | TrkB | Promotes innervation |
| **Neurturin** | Nerves | GFRα2 | Supports neural-tumor crosstalk |

**Key Pathways:**
- **GDNF-RET axis**: CD74 promotes PNI via AKT/EGR-1/GDNF pathway (Zhang et al., 2020)
- **MUC21 phosphorylation**: GDNF-induced, activates RAC2 GTPase driving EMT (Chen et al., 2024)
- **NGF-TrkA signaling**: Mediates pain and metabolic reprogramming (Trentini et al., 2024)

#### 3.1.2 Axon Guidance Molecules

| Molecule | Receptor | Function |
|----------|----------|----------|
| SEMA3D | PLXND1 | Promotes PNI and metastasis |
| Annexin A2 | - | Regulates SEMA3D secretion |
| Netrins | DCC/UNC5 | Directional migration |

The SEMA3D-PLXND1 axis was identified as a key driver of PNI in orthotopic mouse models (Jurcak et al., 2019).

#### 3.1.3 Chemokines and Cytokines

| Pathway | Components | Mechanism |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| CXCL12/CXCR4 | CAF-derived circKLHL24 regulates | Chemotaxis |
| CCL2-CCR4 | TGF-α induced | Human-like NI in mouse models |
| Substance P/NK1R | MMP1/PAR1 loop | Early PNI, pain |

### 3.2 Stromal Contributions

#### Cancer-Associated Fibroblasts (CAFs)

CAFs are emerging as critical players in PNI:

1. **PNI-associated CAFs (pCAFs)** create a high-lactate microenvironment driving neural invasion (Li et al., 2025)
2. **CAF-derived circRNAs** (circKLHL24) promote PNI through CXCL12 regulation (Li et al., 2025)
3. **Activated stroma** is enriched in neuro-invasive samples (Yıldızhan et al., 2025)

#### Schwann Cells

Schwann cells actively support PNI through:
- TGFβ signaling enhancing cancer cell migration and invasion (Roger et al., 2019)
- Extracellular vesicle-mediated communication (Wong et al., 2025)
- Dedifferentiation to a "repair" phenotype guiding tumor cells (He et al., 2025)

### 3.3 Emerging Molecular Insights

**Spatial Transcriptomics (2025):**
- Endocannabinoid metabolism alterations at PNI sites
- Polyamine metabolism dysregulation
- Novel therapeutic targets for cancer and pain (Lakis et al., 2025)

**Circular RNAs:**
- circNFIB promotes PNI via L1CAM/MAPK pathway (Wu et al., 2025)
- circ-0011536 mediates neural remodeling via miR-451a/VGF axis (Dai et al., 2023)

## 4. Neuron Types in PDAC Neural Invasion

### 4.1 Overview of Pancreatic Innervation

The pancreas receives innervation from three major divisions of the peripheral nervous system:

```
Pancreatic Innervation
├── Sympathetic (Adrenergic)
│   ├── Origin: Celiac and superior mesenteric ganglia
│   ├── Neurotransmitter: Norepinephrine
│   └── Effect: PRO-TUMORIGENIC
│
├── Parasympathetic (Cholinergic)
│   ├── Origin: Vagus nerve
│   ├── Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine
│   └── Effect: TUMOR-SUPPRESSIVE
│
└── Sensory (Nociceptive)
    ├── Origin: Dorsal root ganglia (DRG)
    ├── Neurotransmitters: Substance P, CGRP
    └── Effect: PRO-TUMORIGENIC
```

### 4.2 Sympathetic Neurons (Adrenergic)

#### Key Findings

Sympathetic nerves release **norepinephrine (NE)** that promotes PNI and tumor progression:

1. **β-AR/PKA/STAT3 signaling**: NE promotes PNI through this pathway (Guo et al., 2013)
2. **Feedforward loop**: Catecholamines → ADRB2 activation → NGF secretion → increased nerve density → more catecholamine release (Renz et al., 2018)
3. **Chronic stress acceleration**: Stress hormones accelerate PDAC growth (Kim-Fuchs et al., 2014; Partecke et al., 2016)

#### Molecular Mechanisms

| Receptor | Ligand | Downstream Pathway | Effect |
|----------|--------|-------------------|--------|
| β2-AR | NE, Epinephrine | PKA/STAT3 | Promotes invasion, proliferation |
| β2-AR | NE | P38/MAPK | Stimulates migration |
| β1-AR | NE | T cell exhaustion | Immunosuppression |

#### Therapeutic Implications

- β-blocker treatment reduces tumor volumes in preclinical models
- Propranolol enhances response to immunotherapy (Fjæstad et al., 2022)
- Clinical retrospective studies show mixed results (Le Bozec et al., 2023)

### 4.3 Parasympathetic Neurons (Cholinergic)

#### Paradoxical Role - Tumor Suppressive

In contrast to sympathetic signaling, parasympathetic/cholinergic signaling appears to **suppress** PDAC:

| Finding | Reference |
|---------|-----------|
| Subdiaphragmatic vagotomy **accelerates** PDAC development | Renz et al., 2018 |
| Muscarinic agonist bethanechol **restores** normal phenotype | Renz et al., 2018 |
| Cholinergic signaling suppresses cancer stem cells via CHRM1 | Renz et al., 2018 |
| AChE inhibitors slow tumor growth and inflammation | Pfitzinger et al., 2020 |

#### Mechanism

```
Vagus Nerve → Acetylcholine Release
                    ↓
            M1 Muscarinic Receptor
                    ↓
        Suppression of Cancer Stemness
                    ↓
            Reduced Tumor Growth
```

#### Clinical Translation

A Phase 0 study (2025) demonstrated:
- Bethanechol 100mg BID is well-tolerated
- May suppress inflammatory responses in PDAC patients
- Supports further clinical development (White et al., 2025)

#### Complexity

High **M3 receptor** expression paradoxically predicts poor prognosis (Zhang et al., 2016), suggesting differential roles for muscarinic receptor subtypes.

### 4.4 Sensory Neurons (Nociceptive)

#### Critical Role in Tumor Initiation

Sensory neurons from **dorsal root ganglia (DRG)** play a pivotal role:

**Landmark Finding (Saloman et al., 2016):**
- Ablation of sensory neurons in PKC mouse model **slows cancer initiation and progression**
- Sensory denervation reduces inflammation and PanIN formation
- Suggests sensory neurons are required for early PDAC development

#### Nociceptor Neurons (2025 Discovery)

A Cell Research paper revealed that nociceptor neurons promote PDAC through (Wang et al., 2025):

1. **Interaction with CAFs** via CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide)
2. **Suppression of NK cells** - reducing anti-tumor immunity
3. **Direct contribution** to cancer-associated pain

#### Pain Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Mediators | Consequence |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Neurogenic inflammation | Substance P, CGRP | Local tissue damage |
| Central sensitization | Glutamate, SP | Chronic pain |
| Nerve damage | Tumor invasion | Neuropathic pain |

### 4.5 Comparative Summary of Neuron Types

| Neuron Type | Neurotransmitter | Effect on PDAC | Key Receptors | Therapeutic Strategy |
|-------------|------------------|----------------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Sympathetic** | Norepinephrine | Pro-tumorigenic | β2-AR, β1-AR | β-blockers |
| **Parasympathetic** | Acetylcholine | Tumor-suppressive | M1 (protective), M3 (complex) | Muscarinic agonists |
| **Sensory** | Substance P, CGRP | Pro-tumorigenic | NK1R, TRPV1 | Sensory denervation, NK1R antagonists |

### 4.6 Single-Neuron Characterization (2025 Breakthrough)

The "Trace-n-Seq" technology published in Nature (Thiel et al., 2025) represents a paradigm shift:

- First molecular characterization of individual neurons innervating PDAC
- Identified **novel neuronal cell types** specific to tumor innervation
- Revealed molecular networks reprogrammed by cancer
- Supports inclusion of **denervation strategies** in future therapies

## 5. Neural Density and Prognostic Implications

### 5.1 Paradox of Neural Density

Interestingly, **reduced intrapancreatic neural density** in cancer tissue predicts poorer outcomes (Iwasaki et al., 2019):

- Low neural density is an independent prognostic factor
- May reflect aggressive tumor behavior destroying nerves
- Or selection for cancer cells that can survive without neural support

### 5.2 Nerve Development During Carcinogenesis

Peak density of **immature nerve cells** occurs during high-grade dysplasia in IPMNs (Trinh et al., 2022):

- Suggests nerve-tumor interactions are crucial during malignant transformation
- Neural development precedes invasive cancer
- Potential window for early intervention

## 6. Emerging Therapeutic Targets

### 6.1 Current Therapeutic Strategies

| Target | Approach | Stage | Rationale |
|--------|----------|-------|-----------|
| **β-AR signaling** | Propranolol | Clinical trials | Block sympathetic pro-tumor effects |
| **Muscarinic receptors** | Bethanechol | Phase 0 complete | Enhance parasympathetic suppression |
| **NGF/TrkA** | Trk inhibitors | Preclinical | Block neurotrophin feedforward loop |
| **GDNF/RET** | RET inhibitors | Preclinical | Disrupt major PNI axis |
| **Schwann cells** | TGFβ inhibitors | Preclinical | Prevent glial support of invasion |
| **Denervation** | Surgical/chemical | Experimental | Remove neural support entirely |

### 6.2 Combination Strategies

Emerging evidence supports combining neural targeting with:
- Immunotherapy (β-blockers + anti-CTLA4)
- Chemotherapy (Trk inhibitors + gemcitabine)
- Pain management (NK1R antagonists)

## 8. Conclusions

Over the past two decades, our understanding of neural invasion in PDAC has evolved from a histopathological observation to recognition of a complex, bidirectional relationship between cancer and the nervous system.

### Key Insights

1. **PNI is not passive** - Cancer cells actively exploit neural pathways through specific molecular mechanisms

2. **Different neuron types have distinct roles**:
   - Sympathetic neurons: Pro-tumorigenic
   - Parasympathetic neurons: Protective
   - Sensory neurons: Pro-tumorigenic

3. **The neural microenvironment is reprogrammed** - Schwann cells, CAFs, and neurons themselves undergo functional changes that support tumor progression

4. **Therapeutic opportunities exist** - Targeting neural signaling represents a promising complementary approach to conventional therapy

### Clinical Implications

- PNI should be routinely assessed and reported in pathology
- Neural-targeting therapies may complement existing treatments
- Pain management strategies should consider PNI mechanisms
- High-risk patients may benefit from neural-targeted prevention

This field represents a frontier in cancer neuroscience with significant potential for improving outcomes in one of the deadliest malignancies.
